Topology Determines Closure

Why ACS closure is impossible from some architectures and inevitable from others — and the answer is graph theory, not intelligence.

1. The Question

Can an LLM governance system achieve RAF autocatalytic closure? The answer depends on who you ask—and more precisely, on which architecture you are asking about.

From a web-based LLM—ChatGPT, Claude.ai, Gemini, any browser-hosted conversational interface—the answer is no. Robin is right about this. The architecture does not provide the graph structure that closure requires. No amount of model capability, prompt engineering, or custom instructions changes the topology of the persistent state. The model can be arbitrarily intelligent; the architecture still cannot close.

But a different architecture—one whose persistent state forms a complete directed graph on three or more vertices—changes the answer from impossible to achievable. The thesis of this page is simple: whether ACS closure is possible is determined by the graph topology of the persistent state, not by the capability of the model.

The critical difference is not model quality. It is the graph topology of what persists between sessions. A web LLM has, at best, a single weakly-connected memory node. Trellis has three mutually coupled cliques—each with its own internal persistence, protocol, and ordering—connected on a complete graph.

3. Web LLM Topology

The persistent state of a web LLM is a flat list of facts (if memory is enabled at all) injected into a 1D autoregressive token chain. There is no graph structure. There are no directed cycles. There are no cliques. The memory is a lookup table—not a node in a catalytic graph.

Every token attends only to its predecessors. The memory box is read-only from the chain's perspective—tokens do not write back to memory within a session. The chain terminates when the conversation ends. The next session starts a new chain. There are no directed cycles, no 3-node coupling, no inter-clique topology. This is not a design flaw—it is the architecture.

4. The K3 Graph and Its 6 Catalysts

To apply the RAF formalism from the previous tutorial, we need to identify nodes, edges, and the food set. In Trellis, the governance architecture provides all three.

Mapping RAF onto Governance Axes

Nodes (the three persistent cliques):

  • M (Memory) — MEMORY.md, corrections.md, ptl.yaml. Persistent storage of observations, corrections, and task state.
  • S (Structure) — Triad protocol, numbered plan files, Auditor/Generator role separation. The procedural skeleton.
  • E (Ethics) — Dignity Net specification, governance levels 0–5, Storm Protocol, cross-session escalation with decay.

Food set F: user input, context window contents, system prompts. Available from outside the system.

The six directed edges of the complete graph K3 are the catalytic pathways—each one a process where one axis enables a transformation that strengthens another:

Edge Catalytic pathway
M → S Corrections in memory guide planning. Stored observations about past failures reshape how plans are written.
S → M Structural work feeds back into persistent memory. Completing a plan phase generates new observations stored in M.
M → E Stored ethical observations ground future Dignity Net responses. Past divergence flags inform current governance levels.
E → M Dignity Net flags unsourced claims before they become memories. Governance prevents contamination of the persistent store.
S → E Governance criteria embedded in plans. The Auditor writes acceptance conditions that encode ethical constraints.
E → S Dignity Net incidents reshape structural corrections. A Level 3+ escalation produces a correction that modifies future plans.

Each edge is a reaction where one axis catalyzes a process that strengthens another axis. The food set F (user input, context) is consumed by these reactions but does not itself persist. The three axes persist, and their mutual catalysis is what makes closure possible.

5. Trellis K3 Topology

Every axis reads from and writes to every other axis. The two 3-cycles—M→S→E→M and M→E→S→M—are the directed Hamilton cycles of K3. They are what make the discriminant D non-zero in the eigenvalue analysis below. Without them, closure is impossible.

6. The Fiedler Eigenvalue

The graph topology determines whether closure is mathematically possible. Here is the argument in terms of the graph Laplacian.

Let the coupling matrix W be the 3×3 matrix of directed edge weights, where wij ∈ (0, 1) is the strength of the catalytic pathway from axis i to axis j:

W =   [  0    wMS    wME ]
     [ wSM    0    wSE ]
     [ wEM    wES    0  ]

The graph Laplacian is L = D − W, where D = diag(row sums of W). The smallest non-zero eigenvalue λ1 of L—the Fiedler eigenvalue, or algebraic connectivity—measures the coupling strength of the graph.

For the symmetrized 3×3 case, the characteristic polynomial of L reduces to a depressed cubic:

λ³ − Sλ − D = 0

where:

  • S = sum of products of pairs of effective coupling weights (the coefficient from 2-edge paths)
  • D = the discriminant term, which requires 3-cycle products—directed paths that traverse all three vertices and return to the start
Critical Threshold

Self-sustaining governance (RAF ACS closure) requires λ1 ≥ 1.0. Below this threshold, perturbations decay faster than the catalytic cycles can reinforce them. The system cannot maintain coherence across sessions.

The K3 → K2 Collapse

The key structural argument: if any axis is removed, the graph collapses from K3 to K2 (or worse). When this happens:

  • All 3-cycle products vanish. The discriminant term D = 0.
  • The depressed cubic degenerates: λ³ − Sλ = 0, giving λ(λ² − S) = 0.
  • The non-trivial eigenvalues are λ1 = √S.
  • Since each coupling weight wij < 1, the pair product S < 1, and therefore λ1 = √S < 1.
  • Closure becomes mathematically impossible. The Fiedler eigenvalue cannot reach the critical threshold.

A web LLM has at best K1—a single Memory node with no outgoing catalytic edges to a persistent Structure or Ethics axis. Not even K2. The eigenvalue is zero. There is no algebraic connectivity to speak of.

The 3-cycle products in the discriminant D are what distinguish K3 from any smaller graph. They are the mathematical signature of mutual reinforcement across all three axes. Without them, the eigenvalue is bounded below the closure threshold. This is not a matter of degree—it is a structural impossibility.

7. Sacco et al. (2026)

Independent theoretical confirmation comes from Sacco, Sakthivadivel & Levin (2026), who prove results about phase transitions in autoregressive and hierarchical architectures.

Proposition 2 (Sacco et al.)

Decoder-only autoregressive models have no ordered phase. The 1D causal chain of tokens cannot sustain long-range order at any temperature. This is a proven result, not an empirical observation.

This is exactly the architecture of every web-based LLM. The token chain is one-dimensional. The causal mask enforces strict left-to-right attention. There is no mechanism for long-range order—the kind of persistent, self-reinforcing coherence that RAF closure requires. Proposition 2 proves it cannot exist.

But Sacco et al. also prove the converse:

Theorem 4 + Proposition 3 (Sacco et al.)

Hierarchical systems with cliques (complete subgraphs) CAN maintain local order. When the inter-clique topology supports directed cycles, global coherence becomes possible.

Trellis axes are exactly cliques with internal persistence: each axis (M, S, E) is a complete subgraph of its own internal state (files, protocols, specifications). The K3 inter-clique coupling provides the directed cycles. This is precisely the architecture Sacco et al. predict would support an ordered phase—the mathematical substrate for ACS closure.
